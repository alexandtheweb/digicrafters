The Basics

Working with the Arduino board

In the Blue Kit, you'll find an Arduino Uno board, a black USB cable that powers it, a twisty switch called a potentiometer, and A variety of little LED lights (called Light Emitting Diodes - a diode is a 2 legged thing). The Arduino board is a small circuit board. It has a small chip on it (called a microcontroller) that can be used to add interactivity to projects. Arduinos are easy to program, using your normal computer, but you'll need this software to help your computer talk in a way the Arduino will understand.

Once your computer has the software, you can plug the Arduino into it with it's black power cable, and load your Arduino with it's instructions (by uploading sketches). It can be programmed to do many different things, using input from sensors and switches, or controlling output to lights, screens or computers. There are so many possibilities for projects with the Arduino, and Digicrafters have a few to get you started.

In the first project from your Blue Kit, you create a flashing LED. There's a small program, built into the Arduino computer software, which when loaded to the board, turns the power on and off. Just plug in a bright LED to the Arduino and "FLASH!" Let there be light! The "Glowing Gremlin Egg" is Project 2 in the Blue Kit. It uses the Arduino board, it's cable, and a little bit of code that fades the power to the LED up and down. The more power the LED receives the brighter it is, the less power it receives the dimmer it is. This simple idea is the basis of a strange and mystical fantasy foetus. Project 3 takes the egg light show one step further, giving you the power with a colour changing LED controlled by a switch. What will your magic egg hatch?

About us

DigiCrafters is a series of learning, discovery and making projects intended to introduce secondary school students to physical computing. The projects were designed to give anyone a fun, easy and creative way to discover computer sensing and I/O.

Who are we?

We are three banana-wielding MSc students, learning about human-computer interaction at the University of Sussex. In the Spring of 2013, as part of a module called technology-enhanced learning environments, we worked with the students and staff at the Self-Managed Learning College to discover the best way to introduce kids to sensors, microprocessors and circuits.

Andy Finn, Alex Goluszko, Marie Wilkins
